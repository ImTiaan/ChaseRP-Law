# Case Laws

## Carroll v United States
*Defined on January 1, 2026*

The motor vehicle exception allows officers to search a vehicle without a search warrant if they have probable cause to believe that evidence or contraband is in the vehicle.

---

## Tennessee v. Garner
*Defined on January 6, 2026*

A police officer may use deadly force to prevent the escape of a fleeing suspect if the officer has a good faith belief that the suspect poses a immediate risk of death or injury to an officer or others.

---

## Rags v. San Andreas
*Defined on December 29, 2025*

An officer who has a reasonable belief that a person has been engaged in one of the regulated activities of hunting or fishing may search persons, containers and proximate vehicles to inspect their catch or haul, anywhere in the State.

---

## Doegood v. San Andreas
*Defined on December 29, 2025*

A person who renders emergency assistance to an individual may not be sued for their actions unless those actions are negligent.

---

## Castle v. San Andreas
*Defined on December 29, 2025*

Self-defense is an accepted defense that allows a defendant to use physical force to protect oneself from injury or death. Four elements are required for self-defense: (1) in response to an unprovoked attack, (2) which threatens imminent injury or death. The response must use (3) an objectively reasonable degree of force, and (4) the defendant must report the defensive act to law enforcement as soon as its safe to do so.

## Illinois v. Wardlow
*Defined on December 29, 2025*

Unprovoked flight or evasive behavior from an identifiable police officer, as well as being in a high crime area provides enough reasonable suspicion for a Terry stop.

---

## Minnesota v. Dickerson
*Defined on December 29, 2025*

When a police officer who is conducting a lawful pat down search for weapons feels something that plainly is contraband, the object may be seized even though it is not a weapon.

---

## Wyoming v. Houghton
*Defined on December 29, 2025*

The warrantless search of a passenger's container capable of holding the object of a search for which there is probable cause is not a violation of the Fourth Amendment to the United States Constitution because it is justified under the automobile exception as an effect of the car.

---

## Terry v. Ohio
*Defined on December 29, 2025*

Officers can do a pat down on a person they believe has committed, is committing or about to commit a crime, and has a reason to believe that the suspect may be armed and dangerous.

---

## Miranda v. Arizona
*Defined on December 29, 2025*

Statements made in response to direct questioning may not be used for or against you in trial unless you have been advised of your Fifth Amendment rights against self-incrimination. LEOs do not have to read your Miranda Rights if they do not have questions for you.

---

## Maryland v. King
*Defined on December 29, 2025*

Minimally-invasive DNA collection as a part of routine booking procedures for severe crimes is not a violation of the 4th amendment.

---

## Pennsylvania v. Mimms
*Defined on December 5, 1977*

On a traffic stop police can order a driver and any passengers out at any time. Passengers are also considered detained, and may not leave until released. A Terry Frisk under the Reasonable Suspicion of a traffic stop alongside the belief of being armed and dangerous is allowed.
